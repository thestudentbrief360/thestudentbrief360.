Louder Than Silence: The Crisis and Resilience of Afghan Women

By Avika Tase • 06/30/2026
“Today, in Kabul, a female cat has more freedom than a woman.”

Meryl Streep, a Hollywood actress, made this statement during a United Nations General Assembly event. She explained that a cat may sit on its front stoop, feel the sun on its face, or chase a squirrel into a park, while women and girls in Afghanistan have been denied many of these basic freedoms.

This statement reflects the harsh reality that women and girls face in Afghanistan. They are forced to wear a burqa, a clothing item that covers a woman's entire body and only has a mesh opening for the eyes, whenever they step outside of their home.

Women are forbidden from speaking in public, as if they were meant to be invisible. They cannot travel, work, or access public parks or medical care without being accompanied by their husband or another male relative.

Afghanistan is the only country where girls are banned from having a formal education. They are denied access to education beyond sixth grade and are forbidden from attending universities.

Once they reach puberty, some girls' families arrange marriages for them. Girls may be used as a form of currency by being married in exchange for money. Some girls as young as ten years old have been forced to marry men who are decades older than them.

However, these laws have not always been in place.

During the early reforms from 1919 to 1929, King Amanullah and Queen Soraya Tarzi introduced major reforms, including the right to vote, the opening of the first girls' school, funding for new universities, and the end of mandatory burqa use.

Unfortunately, these reforms were later removed after Amanullah was deposed and King Zahir Shah took his place.

During the reign of King Zahir Shah, women achieved greater societal freedom during a period considered by many to be a Golden Age from the 1950s through the 1970s.

During this era, gender segregation was abolished, and the 1964 Constitution allowed women to vote, run for office, and work as professionals, doctors, and judges. In 1965, Aziza Garadazi and Homeira Seljuqi were appointed as the first female senators.

This progressive era ended once the civil war began.

As the Communist Era began in 1978, the Soviet-backed government promoted education for women. However, widespread conflict led to severe violence and the erosion of rights in rural areas.

When the Mujahideen took over in 1992, a civil war broke out. This war caused women to face severe new restrictions on their movement.

During the period from 1996 to 2001, the Taliban first came into power and began enforcing extremely strict laws. These laws banned women from employment and education, severely restricted freedom of movement, and made the burqa mandatory.

After the fall of the Taliban, the Democratic Era began. The new 2004 Constitution guaranteed women's rights and reserved 25% of parliamentary seats for women.

Girls were allowed to return to school and universities, and women became visible and active members of public, political, and cultural life.

This period continued until the Taliban regained power in August 2021.

Ever since the Taliban returned, women and girls have faced severe restrictions on their human rights. They must follow strict laws or risk being killed or imprisoned.

A clear example of these punishments is shown in the short film Burqa City, directed by Fabrice Bracq. In the film, a woman walks into a store and greets the shopkeeper without a male guardian accompanying her. The shopkeeper follows her and blows a whistle, signaling the police.

She is arrested on the spot, despite being fully covered. Although this example comes from a short film, it reflects the very real consequences that women may face under strict restrictions.

Regardless of these consequences, women and activists continue to protest and demand their human rights.

Some protests include marches, defying dress codes, and directly challenging the Taliban. Some protesters have also used creative tactics, such as writing songs.

In October 2002, a band of three anonymous women in Afghanistan called the Burka Band wrote a song called Burka Blue. The indie rock song discussed the struggles of wearing the burqa and became popular internationally.

The song helped spread awareness about the problems facing women in Afghanistan and continues to be remembered as an example of resistance through music.

The Taliban, however, has taken drastic measures to prevent and stop these protests. Many activists have been arrested, harassed, killed, tortured, kidnapped, and assaulted during protests.

Despite these actions, many women continue to resist. They choose not to allow these actions to silence them and remain determined to fight until these laws are abolished.

To conclude, women and girls in Afghanistan are experiencing a human rights crisis. They have been stripped of many of their rights and silenced.

However, their protests continue to grow stronger with the help of people spreading awareness. The fight continues today and will not stop until equality is achieved.
