Abortion Should Be Legal In All States

By Avika Tase
May 26, 2026
Restrictions on abortion access raise questions about bodily autonomy, healthcare access, and whether limiting legal access changes outcomes or simply shifts care into less safe environments.

Abortion should remain legal in all states because access to reproductive healthcare represents an issue of bodily autonomy and individual choice. Supporters argue that governments should not determine personal medical decisions that directly affect someone's future, health, and ability to make decisions regarding their own body.

Human rights organizations have argued that access to abortion intersects with broader rights including privacy, equality, healthcare access, and reproductive autonomy. The position presented in this brief is that restrictions on legal access can create broader consequences beyond the procedure itself.

Legal restrictions do not eliminate demand. They may instead change where and how care is accessed.

The brief also argues that barriers including cost, travel requirements, and provider availability may increase reliance on unsafe alternatives. According to public health organizations, unsafe abortion practices can carry significant medical risks.

Supporters further argue that unequal access places disproportionate burdens on individuals who lack financial resources or live in areas with more restrictive policies.

Health and Broader Impacts

Beyond physical outcomes, abortion policy may also affect emotional wellbeing, financial stability, and long term life planning. Supporters of broader access argue that legal availability provides safer medical environments and greater healthcare consistency.

The article also discusses concerns including anxiety, isolation, and uncertainty that can arise when individuals face barriers to reproductive healthcare.

Counterpoint

Opponents may argue that abortion policy should remain under state authority and reflect local legal frameworks and community priorities. The debate continues to involve questions of ethics, public policy, and healthcare access.
