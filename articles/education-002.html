We Need Early Education Urgently

By Lilli Ahlers • 07/05/26

Early Childhood Education (ECE) is defined as the formal education and care of young children, typically ages 0–5 years old. This type of education takes place in daycares, preschools, and sometimes home-based centers before children begin primary school. Education in these settings is based on the understanding that the early years are a critical period for the development of skills, attitudes, and learning patterns.

There are many long-term benefits for children who attend early childhood education programs. Improved school readiness, higher rates of high school graduation and college attendance, and improved social behaviors are some examples. ECE services also provide an important support system for families as they work to develop and nurture their children's growth.

Early education is not simply childcare. It helps build the foundation for a child's future.

Across the country, there is a lack of ECE services for low-income families. A lack of these services is often referred to as a “childcare desert,” where there are not enough available slots for every child who needs care.

This creates a cycle of disadvantages that society continues to perpetuate. Children who do not attend early childhood education may face greater challenges throughout their academic careers. Education is a key steppingstone toward obtaining a high-paying job, living in a healthy environment, and building a stable social life.

There is a loss of potential that can have a ripple effect on individuals and society. Educators, parents, and other professionals have been sounding the alarm for years, yet policymakers, politicians, and other individuals in positions of power have not fully solved this systemic issue.

The problem has been made worse by a loss of federal funding, high staff burnout and turnover, and low wages for early childhood educators.

One example of this issue can be seen at Oregon State University, a four-year university located in Oregon. Across OSU-affiliated campuses, including Corvallis, Newport, Bend, and Portland, two prominent early education centers have closed.

At the OSU-Cascades campus in Bend, Oregon, the early education center Little Kits recently closed. This closure heavily impacted OSU educators, employees, and students.

The daycare had a dual purpose. First, it provided much-needed daycare slots for parents. Second, it provided training opportunities for future educators as part of the university's Early Childhood Education program.

OSU-Cascades is located in Central Oregon, an area with high childcare costs, especially for infant and toddler care. The closure of Little Kits demonstrates how important early childhood education centers are for parents, students, educators, and communities.

A lack of affordable and available childcare resources can heavily strain families and communities. This can contribute to high stress, loss of employment and income, and reduced social interaction.

Structured ECE policies, depending on how they are implemented, can help lessen the burden on parents. However, many individuals argue that ECE educators and professionals are simply glorified babysitters and that parents should stay home to care for their children.

While this argument exists throughout the country, it is not realistic for many families. With the cost of living being so high, having one parent stay home full-time is not financially possible for many households.

Families need accessible and affordable options that support both children and parents.

ECE educators and professionals are hard-working individuals who help build foundational skills for the next generation. There needs to be an alternative option that parents can easily access and use.

Applications should be simple to understand and complete, and income eligibility for low-income parents should be broader. Expanding access to early childhood education is an important step toward supporting children, families, and communities.
